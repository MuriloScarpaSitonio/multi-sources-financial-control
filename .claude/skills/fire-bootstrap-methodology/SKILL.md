# FIRE Bootstrap Methodology

This skill captures the design decisions behind the FIRE bootstrap simulation in `react/src/pages/private/Home/fireBootstrap.ts`, `react/src/pages/private/Home/ConstantDollarIndicator.tsx`, and `react/src/pages/private/Home/ConstantDollarAgeInBondsIndicator.tsx`. Read this before changing the bootstrap logic, the success-rate display, or how either Constant-Dollar indicator gates its outputs.

## What the bootstrap computes

For each of N (default 2000) trials, simulate `horizon` years of retirement:

1. Start with `effectivePatrimony` (current patrimony, optionally overridden by the PatrimonySimulator).
2. Each year:
   - Draw a random *real* return from the historical distribution (IBOV 1995â€“2025, IFIX 2011â€“2025, CDI 1995â€“2025), blended by the user's actual portfolio allocation (`equityTotal`, `ifixTotal`, `fixedIncomeTotal + bankAmount`).
   - `grown = balance Ã— (1 + r)`; `actualWithdrawal = min(annualWithdrawal, max(0, grown))`; `balance = grown âˆ’ actualWithdrawal`. The `min` cap ensures a depleting trial's withdrawal that year is its remaining grown balance, not the full requested amount.
   - Mark depletion year if balance hits zero.
3. After all trials, compute:
   - **Success rate**: fraction of trials where balance never hit zero within the horizon.
   - **Balance bands** (`bands`, p10/p50/p90): per-year quantiles of balance across all trials.
   - **Withdrawal bands** (`withdrawalBands`, p10/p50/p90): per-year quantiles of `actualWithdrawal` across trials. Stays flat at `annualWithdrawal` while solvent; drops to 0 once a trial depletes. Used by the `DrawdownTooltipContent` to display "balance Â· monthly income" alongside each percentile.
   - **Depletion percentiles** (`medianDepletionYear`, `p10DepletionYear`): both computed over **all** trials (success treated as `+Infinity`), so the two share a denominator and stay coherent (no more "median 56 / p10 nunca" pairs). Either is `null` when the corresponding percentile is `+Infinity`.

The historical real-returns dataset is generated by `django/variable_income_assets/scripts.py::generate_fire_returns_ts` and lives in `react/src/pages/private/Home/fireReturns.ts`. The script pulls B3 IBOV historical levels, B3 IFIX historical levels, BCB SGS 4391 (CDI accumulated monthly), and BCB SGS 433 (IPCA monthly), aggregates to annual returns, and deflates nominal returns by IPCA: `real = (1 + nominal) / (1 + IPCA) âˆ’ 1`.

## What runs at each render

### Why both lifestyle and rate-based bootstraps coexist

Earlier iterations of this skill described a "Trinity (chosen) vs Lifestyle (rejected)" decision. **That framing is no longer accurate.** Today both bootstraps are computed and surface as supporting indicators because they answer different questions:

- **Lifestyle (`bootstrap`)** answers the planning question: *"can my actual money sustain my actual life?"* â€” the only sensible framing for the post-FIRE drawdown chart, since by then the user *is* withdrawing their real expenses.
- **Rate test (`rateBootstrap`)** answers the literature-comparable SWR question: *"is X% historically safe at this horizon?"* â€” scale-invariant by construction so it can flag aggressive rate choices independent of patrimony size.

Don't collapse them back into one. The bar's driver (the horizon-stretched multiplier `fireTarget = expenses Ã— (100/userRate) Ã— max(1, horizonFactor)`) is a third, separate construct â€” see "Design history" below for what was tried and rejected before landing here.

## What the progress bar shows

**Bar = `patrimony / fireTarget Ã— 100`** â€” the user's mental model is "fraction of money I need to retire."

`fireTarget = annualExpenses Ã— baseMultiplier Ã— horizonFactor` where:

- `baseMultiplier = 100 / userRate` â€” the rule-of-thumb FIRE multiplier at the user's chosen rate (4% â†’ 25Ã—, 3% â†’ 33Ã—).
- `horizonFactor = max(1, safeRate(30, weights) / safeRate(horizon, weights))` â€” bootstrap-derived stretch for long horizons, clamped at 1 from below. At 30y factor = 1 (Trinity baseline). At 60y factor â‰ˆ 1.4 (need ~40% more to survive sequence risk over a longer span). At horizons < 30y the raw ratio drops below 1, but we clamp so `fireTarget` never shrinks below the rule-of-thumb floor `expenses Ã— 100/userRate` â€” see "Why the clamp" below.

Sliders move the bar:
- **Rate up** â†’ smaller `baseMultiplier` â†’ smaller target â†’ bar up.
- **Horizon up from 30y** â†’ bigger `horizonFactor` â†’ bigger target â†’ bar down.
- **Horizon down from 30y** â†’ factor stays at 1 (clamp) â†’ bar unchanged. Intentional: the cash-flow constraint at the chosen rate is the binding floor; no point pretending you need less money than the rule-of-thumb says.
- **Patrimony up** â†’ numerator up â†’ bar up.

### Design history (avoid re-litigating)

The user's mental model: bar = "fraction of money I need to retire" â€” i.e., `patrimony / FIRE_number`. They want both sliders to monotonically move the bar via FIRE_number, with no asymmetric behavior. We landed on horizon-stretched multiplier scaling after rejecting:

- **Option A**: `target = expenses Ã— (100 / userRate)`. Horizon didn't move the bar.
- **Option B-old (rate as what-if)**: `target = expenses Ã— (100 / safeRate)`. Rate slider inert.
- **Option C-cap**: `target = expenses Ã— (100 / min(userRate, safeRate))`. Asymmetric â€” slider rate or horizon dominated depending on which side of safeRate.
- **Framing B with rate-based bootstrap** (bar = bootstrap success at `userRate Ã— patrimony`): scale-invariant, patrimony didn't move the bar (user reported "96% success but I need to double my money").
- **Framing B with lifestyle bootstrap** (bar = bootstrap success at `annualExpenses`): rate slider didn't move the bar.
- **Composite** (`bar = targetCoverage Ã— lifestyleSuccess Ã— 100`): all three drivers moved the bar, but the bar's value didn't match the user's mental model of "fraction of FIRE_number" (a 58%-of-target user saw 33% on the bar because the success rate factor dragged it down).

**Current approach** â€” bar = patrimony / fireTarget, with `fireTarget = expenses Ã— (100/userRate) Ã— max(1, horizonFactor)`. The tooltip on the bar shows the *combined* multiplier ("Meta de FIRE pela regra X%: ZÃ— despesas anuais"); the rate and horizon sliders next to the bar are where the user sees the two components separately. (Showing the breakdown explicitly â€” e.g. "regra 4% Ã— ajuste 60a = 35Ã—" â€” was discussed but not built; the indicator only displays the final multiplier today.) The clamp at 1 from below was added after a user-reported bug where the bar read 118% while the cash-flow line said "faltam R$ 672/mÃªs" â€” see "Why the clamp" above. Above 30y both sliders monotonically move the bar via fireTarget; below 30y the horizon slider intentionally doesn't move it because the rule-of-thumb floor binds. Patrimony always moves the bar via the numerator.

**Do not** revert to a static-multiplier or a bootstrap-success bar. Both fail the user's mental model. The horizon-stretched multiplier is transparent (visible in the UI) and produces the expected behavior at every operating point.

## Lifestyle gap is shown separately

The bar conveys "fraction of the FIRE_number I have", but it doesn't directly show whether the chosen *rate's withdrawal* covers the user's expenses today. That gap surfaces in the cash-flow line right under the bar:

## Determinism

`fireBootstrap.ts` uses **Mulberry32** seeded with a fixed constant (`FIXED_SEED = 42`), not `Math.random()`. This is intentional:

- Same inputs (patrimony, withdrawal, horizon, weights) â†’ identical bootstrap output across reloads. Users don't see jitter.
- A fixed seed means slider movements test the *same* 2000 sampled return sequences at different parameters â†’ smoother, more interpretable transitions in success rate.
- Using a hash of inputs as the seed was considered and rejected for the same reason â€” it would make small slider changes scramble the random sequences, causing non-monotonic jumps in the success rate.

## Time-varying-weights variant (Idade em RF)

`fireBootstrap.ts` exposes a second pair of entry points â€” `runBootstrapWithVaryingWeights` and `findSafeWithdrawalRateWithVaryingWeights` â€” that take a `weightsAt: (yearIndex: number) => AllocationWeights` callback instead of a fixed `AllocationWeights` object. Internally the function precomputes `weightsByYear` and `availableByYear` once via `weightsAt` and `availableYearIndices`, then the trial loop calls `drawAlignedYearReturn` with the year-`y-1` weights and available indices to produce one calendar-year-aligned blended return (same sampling rules as the static path, just per-year weights). Everything else (Mulberry32 seeded with `FIXED_SEED`, depletion bookkeeping, percentile bands, scale-invariance trick used by the SWR finder) is identical to the static-weight version.

`ConstantDollarAgeInBondsIndicator.tsx` is the only consumer today. The strategy says "bond% = age" â€” at year `y`, `bondPct = min(currentAge + y, 100) / 100` and `stockPct = 1 - bondPct`. The stock-side split between `equity` and `ifix` preserves the user's current equity:ifix ratio, since the strategy only dictates the bond/stock split and doesn't speak to which equity vehicle to hold.

### Why time-varying weights are the right model here

The age-in-bonds glide path is *intrinsic* to the strategy definition â€” bond% = age is what makes it the strategy, not an incidental allocation choice. Modeling it with the static `runBootstrap` would mean picking a single bond% (today's age, retirement-age, average over the horizon, etc.), and any choice misrepresents what the user is actually doing:

- Use today's age â†’ simulation is too aggressive late in retirement (real allocation has crept toward bonds, but the bootstrap keeps drawing equity at young-person weights).
- Use end-of-horizon age â†’ too defensive early on (the bootstrap drawn under heavy bond weights despite the user actually holding mostly equities in the early years).
- Use a midpoint â†’ both errors at once, smaller in magnitude.

Time-varying weights eliminate the choice. The bootstrap traces the same glide path the user will actually live, which is what gives the success rate an honest interpretation.

### Caveats specific to `findSafeWithdrawalRateWithVaryingWeights`

- The "safe rate" is **trajectory-dependent**, not just horizon- and weights-dependent. Two users with the same horizon and the same starting weights but different glide-path slopes get different safe rates. So does the same user at different starting ages â€” a 35-year-old's 30-year safe rate under age-in-bonds is *not* the same as a 65-year-old's 30-year safe rate under age-in-bonds, because the average bond% over the simulation differs.
- This means the safe rate cannot be cached by `(horizon, weights)` like it can in the static case. The closure captured by `weightsAt` is part of the cache key. The indicator memoizes by `[currentAge, equityTotal, ifixTotal, targetYears]` instead.
- `horizonFactor = baselineSafeRate / safeRate` (used for `fireTarget`) still works the same way â€” both endpoints are computed via the time-varying SWR finder, and the ratio still answers "how much do I need to stretch the rule-of-thumb multiplier to survive this horizon?". The numerical magnitudes shift compared to a static-weight portfolio, but the slider semantics (longer horizon â†’ bigger target, lower rate â†’ bigger target) are preserved.
- The 30-year baseline is still anchored at `findSafeWithdrawalRateWithVaryingWeights(30, weightsAt)`, *not* at the static-weight baseline. Mixing them would produce a horizon factor of 1 at the wrong operating point and break monotonicity of the bar.

## Accumulation-phase variant (time-to-target forecast)

`runAccumulationBootstrap` is the forward-running counterpart to the withdrawal variants. Starting from `startingBalance`, each simulated year it adds `annualContribution`, then applies one historical real return drawn at the portfolio `weights`, and stops when the running balance crosses `target`. Used by `ConstantDollarIndicator` and `ConstantDollarAgeInBondsIndicator` to answer the user's planning question: *"given my current patrimony and average monthly savings, how long until I reach my FIRE target?"*

Result shape: `{ successRate, medianYearsToTarget, p10YearsToTarget, p90YearsToTarget, gapBands }`. `successRate` is the fraction of trials that crossed `target` within `maxYears` (default 60). Trials that never reach are excluded from the year-to-target percentile aggregates. `gapBands` are per-year percentiles of `gap = max(0, target âˆ’ balance)` across trials â€” bands all converge to 0 by `p90YearsToTarget` and stay there. The consuming accumulation chart slices around `p90YearsToTarget + small_buffer`; past that everything is at 0.

### Inverted percentile semantics

Watch out â€” the p10/p90 conventions flip relative to the depletion variants:

| Variant | p10 means | p90 means |
|---|---|---|
| Withdrawal depletion (`runBootstrap`) | year by which 10% of trials have depleted (success = +âˆž in the sort, so this is `null` when â‰¥90% survive) â€” earliest failures | unused; we report `medianDepletionYear` for the central case |

### How the indicator wires it

The FIRE indicators receive `monthlySavings` as a prop (typically `revenuesAvg âˆ’ expensesAvg` from the existing 12-month rolling averages). They convert to `annualContribution = max(0, monthlySavings) Ã— 12` and pass the same `weights` they use for the withdrawal bootstrap. Three UI states:

### Allocation in Idade-em-RF: static accumulation, glide-path drawdown

`ConstantDollarAgeInBondsIndicator` uses the user's *current* allocation (`accumulationWeights`) for the accumulation bootstrap, not the time-varying glide path. This is a deliberate honesty trade-off: pre-retirement, the user is still working and hasn't started rebalancing toward bonds, so today's mix is the correct input. If we instead drove accumulation through the age-glide, we'd be modeling allocations the user isn't actually holding yet. Documented here so future-you doesn't "fix" it.

The post-FIRE lifestyle bootstrap (`bootstrap`, the one consumed by the depletion labels and the post-FIRE chart) uses `runBootstrapWithVaryingWeights` with a `weightsAt` anchored at *current* age. That's correct because a post-FIRE user is retiring *now* â€” the glide starts at today's age.

The pre-FIRE quantities â€” `safeRate`, `baselineSafeRate`, `horizonFactor`, `targetMultiplier`, `fireTarget`, `rateBootstrap`, `accumulation`, `drawdownAtTarget` â€” share a *single* glide-path anchor produced by the fixed-point solver (next section). One anchor across target, tooltip multiplier, "agressivo" warning, and Aposentadoria preview, instead of the earlier code's split where the target was sized at `currentAge` but the preview was anchored at `currentAge + medianYearsToTarget`.

### The fixed-point solver (`solveAgeInBondsFireState`)

The glide-path anchor problem is self-referential: `fireTarget` depends on `safeRate`, which depends on the post-retirement glide path, which depends on retirement age, which depends on accumulation reaching `fireTarget`. For an age-in-bonds user pre-FIRE, there's no closed-form anchor â€” only a fixed point.

The solver iterates:

1. Start with `anchorAge = currentAge`. Build `weightsAt` glided from there.
2. Compute `safeRate`, `baselineSafeRate`, `horizonFactor`, `targetMultiplier`, `fireTarget`, `rateBootstrap`, then run `runAccumulationBootstrap` against that target with the user's static `accumulationWeights`.
3. Read `medianYearsToTarget`. The implied next anchor is `currentAge + medianYearsToTarget`.
4. Test stop conditions; if none triggered, set the anchor to that next value and loop.

After the loop closes, the chosen pass's anchor is also used to compute `drawdownAtTarget` (so the preview chart is on the same glide as the FIRE-target sizing).

**Stop conditions and guards:**

- **`converged`** â€” the iteration's `medianYearsToTarget` equals the prior iterate's. The next anchor would equal the current one; we have a fixed point.
- **`cycle`** â€” the current iterate's median appears in some non-immediately-prior iterate. (The 1-cycle case is handled by `converged`; this branch covers 2+ cycles.) Pick the most conservative member across all visited iterates plus the current pass: largest `fireTarget`, with later `medianYearsToTarget` as a secondary tie-break. The tie-break is secondary because target and median can diverge across cycle members, and the conservative quantity is the target you have to hit. **Cycle is checked before `target_delta`** â€” a 2-cycle like 8 â†’ 10 â†’ 8 can produce a sub-1% fireTarget swing on the third pass, which would otherwise exit as `target_delta` and silently bypass `pickConservative`.
- **`target_delta`** â€” only checked once we have at least three iterates (i.e. `i >= 2`) **and** the cycle check above didn't fire. If `|fireTarget âˆ’ previousFireTarget| / previousFireTarget < 1%`, accept. Single-step sub-1% moves can be coincidental; requiring three iterates makes it a stability signal.
- **`max_iter`** â€” hard cap at `SOLVER_MAX_ITER = 5`. The mapping `anchorAge â†’ fireTarget â†’ medianYearsToTarget` is integer-quantized (medians are whole years) and bootstrap-noisy, so it's not provably contractive (or even monotonic). The cap is load-bearing, not just defensive. **Pick conservative across all visited iterates plus the last pass** â€” the last iterate isn't necessarily the highest-`fireTarget` one, so returning it would silently understate the target.
- **`unreachable`** â€” if any pass produces `accumulation.medianYearsToTarget === null` (target not reached in 60 years), abort immediately, return that pass's other values plus `drawdownAtTarget: null`. **Don't** fall back to the current-age anchor; that would dress an unreachable case up as a coherent-looking projection. The Aposentadoria preview is then skipped at the consumer (existing danger-colored "improvÃ¡vel atingir em 60 anos" hint already covers that branch).

**The returned object** includes everything the indicator needs to render the bar, tooltip, "agressivo" warning, accumulation chart, and Aposentadoria preview from a single source of truth: `{ fireTarget, targetMultiplier, horizonFactor, safeRate, baselineSafeRate, rateBootstrap, accumulation, drawdownAtTarget, anchorAge, status }`. Don't bypass the solver for any of these â€” recomputing them outside the loop reintroduces the split-anchor bug.

**Cost:** ~2â€“5Ã— the per-render bootstrap budget (one pass costs ~1Ã— the static-weight equivalent: two SWR binary searches at 1000 trials each + one rateBootstrap at 2000 + one accumulation at 2000 + one final drawdown at 2000). Post-FIRE the solver converges on the second iterate (median = 0 â†’ next anchor = currentAge â†’ identical to first pass), so it's effectively a no-op there. Pre-FIRE in practice 2â€“3 iterates is typical.

## Sampling: availability-aware aligned-year, size-1 blocks

`drawAlignedYearReturn` (`fireBootstrap.ts`) samples one historical calendar year per simulated year and applies *that year's* per-asset returns to the user's weights. So if the simulator picks 2008, equity, IFIX, and fixed income all draw their 2008 numbers â€” preserving cross-asset correlation under stress (the old IID-per-asset sampler would have paired 2008 equity with arbitrary other-year bonds, which is wrong).

The sample window depends on the user's weights via `availableYearIndices`, gated by an explicit materiality threshold `MIN_WEIGHT_FOR_RETURN_SERIES = 0.005` (0.5%):

- **`weights.ifix >= 0.005`** â†’ restrict to IFIX-available years (2011â€“2025, 15 calendar years). The user has material IFIX exposure; the 2011-onwards sample is the honest one to use because that's the only calendar window for which we have IFIX returns.
- **`weights.ifix < 0.005`** â†’ use the full IBOV/CDI/IPCA window (1995â€“2025, 31 calendar years). Tiny IFIX dust is treated as zero for sample-window purposes.

The threshold isn't cosmetic. We shipped strict `> 0` first and a user reported SWR60 = 1.63% at a 70/30-ish allocation, vs. the ~3% they expected from Trinity. Root cause: the strict `> 0` cliff dropped users from the full sample to the 15-year IFIX sample for any nonzero IFIX, including a single share's worth of dust. That loses years like 1999 (+131% real equity), 2003 (+81%), and 2009 (+75%). The 0.5% threshold means a user holding a single share of one FII in a R$1M portfolio doesn't lose the longer equity/fixed-income history for it.

If you raise or lower the threshold, document the SWR magnitude impact for representative allocations (the dust-cliff incident is a good template). Don't go back to strict `> 0` without evidence that the threshold caused real harm.

The indicators expose this to users via a small italicized hint under the "Limite seguro" line: *"Amostra histÃ³rica: 2011â€“2025 (15 anos) â€” sua exposiÃ§Ã£o a FII restringe a janela. NÃ£o compare diretamente com SWRs Trinity baseados em sÃ©ries longas (US 1926+)."* The disclosure prevents users from interpreting an honest-but-low SWR (driven by Brazilian sample limitations) as an apples-to-apples Trinity comparison.

**Pick the right predicate per indicator** â€” this is load-bearing:

- **Static `ConstantDollarIndicator`** uses `isIfixRestrictedSample(weights)` against the user's static allocation. Weights don't drift over the horizon there, so the static check matches what the simulation actually does.
- **Age-in-bonds `ConstantDollarAgeInBondsIndicator`** uses `isIfixRestrictedSampleForVaryingWeights(weightsAt, horizon)` against the *solver's chosen* `weightsAt` (rebuilt from `solverState.anchorAge`). Why: a user with 0.4% IFIX today (below threshold) but a 100% IFIX:equity ratio sees the simulated glide push `weights.ifix` well above threshold during stock-heavy retirement years, so the simulation actually uses the IFIX-restricted sample even though the static check would say otherwise. Using `isIfixRestrictedSample(accumulationWeights)` here would silently miss exactly the case where users most need the warning.

### "Excluir FII da simulaÃ§Ã£o" toggle

Each indicator exposes a checkbox embedded inside the disclosure region: when checked, the simulator treats IFIX as cash earning 0% real. Implementation rules:

- **Override at the call site, don't renormalize.** `simWeights = excludeIfix ? { ...rawWeights, ifix: 0 } : rawWeights`. The age-in-bonds variant pushes the flag through `buildAgeInBondsWeightsAt(anchorAge, equityTotal, ifixTotal, excludeIfix)`, which sets the per-year `ifix` slot to 0 while keeping `equityRatio` computed from the real portfolio (so the equity slot does *not* absorb the IFIX fraction). Weights then sum to less than 1 by design â€” the missing fraction earns 0% real, which is exactly the "treat as cash" semantics the user picked.
- **No redistribution.** If you renormalize the remaining weights to 1 you've silently turned the toggle into "treat IFIX as equity+FI proportionally," which answers a different question. Same goes for setting `ifixTotal = 0` upstream of `buildAgeInBondsWeightsAt` â€” that drives `equityRatio = 1` and redistributes onto equity. Don't.
- **Sample window unlocks automatically.** Because `simWeights.ifix === 0 < MIN_WEIGHT_FOR_RETURN_SERIES`, `availableYearIndices` returns the full 1995â€“2025 IBOV/CDI/IPCA sample and `isIfixRestrictedSample(simWeights) === false`. No special-casing required.
- **Disclosure region gates on `rawWeights`, not `simWeights`.** Otherwise the checkbox hides itself when checked and the user can't toggle back. Static gates on `isIfixRestrictedSample(rawWeights)`; age-in-bonds gates on `isIfixRestrictedSampleForVaryingWeights(buildAgeInBondsWeightsAt(anchor, equity, ifix, false), targetYears)` â€” note the explicit `false` so the gate reflects the real portfolio, not the override.
- **Both indicators must thread the flag end-to-end.** Static: `simWeights` flows into all four bootstrap calls (`bootstrap`, `rateBootstrap`, `accumulation`, `drawdownAtTarget`). Age-in-bonds: the flag goes into the solver (`excludeIfix` param), into `lifestyleWeightsAt`, and into `accumulationWeights` via the same `{ ...raw, ifix: 0 }` override. Forgetting any one path produces a UI where some quantities reflect the toggle and others don't.

For the time-varying-weight bootstrap, `availableIndices` is precomputed per simulation year (via `availableByYear`) since weights â€” and therefore the active-asset set â€” can change year-by-year. In practice the age-in-bonds glide path holds the equity:ifix ratio fixed, so the active set is constant for each user; the per-year recomputation is correctness insurance, not a real cost.

## Remaining methodology limits worth disclosing

These are real caveats â€” not bugs, just things a future reader (or a reviewer comparing against Bogleheads/Trinity directly) will notice.

1. **Size-1 blocks (no autocorrelation).** Aligned-year sampling preserves *cross-asset* correlation but draws each simulated year's calendar year independently. So 2008-then-2009 sequences appear at the same probability as 2008-then-2024, even though the historical record had 2008 â†’ 2009 specifically. With ~15 IFIX-available years, multi-year blocks would advertise precision we don't have, so size-1 is the deliberate choice. Worth revisiting once the IFIX series is longer or if there's a concrete reason (e.g. modelling bond-price mean reversion).
2. **End-of-year withdrawal.** `runTrial` grows the balance before withdrawing (`grown = balance Ã— (1 + r); balance = grown âˆ’ withdrawal`). This is the canonical Trinity / Bengen formulation, but it's slightly more optimistic than start-of-year ("paycheck") withdrawal as used by some practitioners (e.g. ERN). Don't change the convention without re-deriving the safe-rate cache and spot-checking the SWR values against the literature you're trying to match.
3. **Real returns end-to-end.** We don't model inflation as a separate stochastic process â€” it's already baked into the deflated return series. Better than ad-hoc real adjustment, but it means we can never display *nominal* trajectories ("withdrawal grew to R$X by year 30") without a redesign.
4. **Brazilian sample is short and regime-heavy.** IBOV/CDI/IPCA covers 1995â€“2025 (31 years), while IFIX covers 2011â€“2025 (15 years). Less variety than Trinity's longer US samples; especially thin on the IFIX side, where 15 distinct annual return vectors mean every IFIX-holding user's bootstrap distribution is highly quantized regardless of trial count. The added 1995â€“2000 window also includes post-Plano-Real fixed-income years with unusually high real CDI (e.g. 1995), so don't describe those years as a repeatable forward-looking rate environment. Disclosure-only â€” not an actionable bug, but it's why we don't bother with multi-year blocks.

## Things to preserve when modifying

1. **Both bootstraps stay** â€” `bootstrap` (lifestyle, scale-variant) and `rateBootstrap` (rate test, scale-invariant) answer different questions and are both surfaced in the UI. Don't collapse them; the bar already uses neither, so there's no single-bootstrap design that recovers the current behavior. Earlier iterations of this skill described a "Trinity won, lifestyle was rejected" decision â€” that's outdated; see "Why both lifestyle and rate-based bootstraps coexist" above.
2. **The `fireProgress` threshold gates *which* charts render, not whether the bootstrap runs.** Pre-FIRE we run two bootstraps (accumulation + a hypothetical drawdown seeded at `fireTarget`) and render both charts; post-FIRE we render only the existing `runBootstrap(currentPatrimony, ...)` chart. Don't collapse this: pre-FIRE the user needs the time-to-target story (AcumulaÃ§Ã£o), and the Aposentadoria preview is meaningful precisely *because* it's hypothetical â€” it answers "if I hit the target, will it last?" The old gate ("hide the bootstrap entirely below 100%") is gone and shouldn't be reintroduced; the bootstrap is meaningful in both regimes.
3. **Both `horizonFactor`s clamp at 1 from below.** The static variant (`ConstantDollarIndicator.tsx:278`) and the time-varying variant (`ConstantDollarAgeInBondsIndicator.tsx:298`) both apply `Math.max(1, baselineSafeRate / safeRate)`. Without the clamp, short horizons can shrink `fireTarget` below the rule-of-thumb floor and reopen the "bar 100% but withdrawal short" contradiction documented in "Why the clamp".
4. **Aligned-year sampling stays.** Every asset's return for a simulated year comes from the *same* historical calendar year via `drawAlignedYearReturn`. Don't revert to independent per-asset draws â€” that decorrelates 2008's stress across asset classes and biases success rates upward in stressed regimes. If you need to change sampling (e.g. introduce blocks), preserve the same-year-across-assets invariant. Also keep the `MIN_WEIGHT_FOR_RETURN_SERIES` threshold and the user-facing IFIX-restricted-sample disclosure â€” both exist to fix the dust-cliff incident documented in the "Sampling" section above. Reverting to strict `> 0` halves SWRs for any user holding a single share of any FII.
5. **Single solver call drives all age-in-bonds pre-FIRE quantities.** `safeRate`, `baselineSafeRate`, `horizonFactor`, `targetMultiplier`, `fireTarget`, `rateBootstrap`, `accumulation`, and `drawdownAtTarget` all come out of one `solveAgeInBondsFireState` call sharing one `anchorAge`. **Don't recompute any of these outside the solver** â€” that's how the old split-anchor bug crept in (target sized at currentAge, preview anchored at retirement age). The Aposentadoria preview's age axis must read `solverState.anchorAge`, not `currentAge + accumulation.medianYearsToTarget` (those are equal on convergence but can differ at `max_iter` or `cycle` exits).
6. **Don't reseed per render** â€” reverting to `Math.random()` will reintroduce reload jitter the user complained about.
7. **Bank cash counts as fixed income** â€” call sites pass `fixedIncomeTotal + bankAmount` so the allocation reflects that cash earns ~risk-free real returns, not zero.
8. **`AÃ§Ã£o EUA` and `Cripto` are lumped into the equity bucket** â€” explicit simplification because we don't ship US equity / crypto historical real-return series. Document any change to this in the call sites in `Indicators.tsx`, `StrategyDetailPage.tsx`, `PlanningHub.tsx`.
9. **Data refresh**: re-running `generate_fire_returns_ts` overwrites `fireReturns.ts`. The script lives in `django/variable_income_assets/scripts.py` and is callable from the Django shell.
10. **Don't collapse the time-varying variant into the static one** â€” they look similar but the static version *cannot* be safely substituted for an age-in-bonds-style strategy (see "Why time-varying weights are the right model here"). Keep both pairs of functions side by side. If you need a third strategy with its own glide path, add a new `weightsAt` callback at the call site rather than threading another parameter through the static-weight path.
