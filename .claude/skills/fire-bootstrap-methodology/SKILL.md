# FIRE Bootstrap Methodology

This skill captures the design decisions behind the FIRE bootstrap simulation in `react/src/pages/private/Home/fireBootstrap.ts`, `react/src/pages/private/Home/ConstantDollarIndicator.tsx`, and `react/src/pages/private/Home/ConstantDollarAgeInBondsIndicator.tsx`. Read this before changing the bootstrap logic, the success-rate display, or how either Constant-Dollar indicator gates its outputs.

## What the bootstrap computes

For each of N (default 2000) trials, simulate `horizon` years of retirement:

1. Start with `effectivePatrimony` (current patrimony, optionally overridden by the PatrimonySimulator).
2. Each year:
   - Draw a random *real* return from the historical distribution (NEFIN equity 2001–2025, IFIX 2011–2025, NEFIN risk-free 2001–2025), blended by the user's actual portfolio allocation (`equityTotal`, `ifixTotal`, `fixedIncomeTotal + bankAmount`).
   - `grown = balance × (1 + r)`; `actualWithdrawal = min(annualWithdrawal, max(0, grown))`; `balance = grown − actualWithdrawal`. The `min` cap ensures a depleting trial's withdrawal that year is its remaining grown balance, not the full requested amount.
   - Mark depletion year if balance hits zero.
3. After all trials, compute:
   - **Success rate**: fraction of trials where balance never hit zero within the horizon.
   - **Balance bands** (`bands`, p10/p50/p90): per-year quantiles of balance across all trials.
   - **Withdrawal bands** (`withdrawalBands`, p10/p50/p90): per-year quantiles of `actualWithdrawal` across trials. Stays flat at `annualWithdrawal` while solvent; drops to 0 once a trial depletes. Used by the `DrawdownTooltipContent` to display "balance · monthly income" alongside each percentile.
   - **Depletion percentiles** (`medianDepletionYear`, `p10DepletionYear`): both computed over **all** trials (success treated as `+Infinity`), so the two share a denominator and stay coherent (no more "median 56 / p10 nunca" pairs). Either is `null` when the corresponding percentile is `+Infinity`.

The historical real-returns dataset is generated by `django/variable_income_assets/scripts.py::generate_fire_returns_ts` and lives in `react/src/pages/private/Home/fireReturns.ts`. The script pulls NEFIN's daily factor CSV, B3's published IFIX yearly variation, and BCB SGS series 433 (IPCA monthly), aggregates daily to annual via compounding, and deflates nominal returns by IPCA: `real = (1 + nominal) / (1 + IPCA) − 1`.

## Why Trinity-style framing (not "lifestyle sustainability")

There are two coherent ways to ask the FIRE question:

| Framing | Withdrawal in simulation | Slider drives | Tells you... |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Trinity (chosen)** | `patrimony × rate%` | the simulation directly | "is X% withdrawal historically safe at my horizon and allocation?" |
| **Lifestyle (rejected)** | `current annual expenses` | only the FIRE-target progress bar | "can my portfolio sustain my current lifestyle right now?" |

We chose Trinity because:

1. It's the canonical SWR research framing (Bengen 1994, Trinity 1998, Pfau, Kitces, ERN). The success rate is directly comparable to FIRE-literature numbers.
2. The withdrawal-rate slider becomes a meaningful planning input — moving 4% → 3% drops simulated withdrawal and raises success, exactly as SWR research predicts.
3. The "Retirada vs Despesas" line is still rendered, so the lifestyle gap is *visible* even when the simulation is rate-driven. Users see whether the chosen rate actually covers their spending.

The cost is that the success rate doesn't directly answer "can I retire now with my current expenses?" — that's why we gate it (next section).

## What the progress bar shows

**Bar = `patrimony / fireTarget × 100`** — the user's mental model is "fraction of money I need to retire."

`fireTarget = annualExpenses × baseMultiplier × horizonFactor` where:

- `baseMultiplier = 100 / userRate` — the rule-of-thumb FIRE multiplier at the user's chosen rate (4% → 25×, 3% → 33×).
- `horizonFactor = max(1, safeRate(30, weights) / safeRate(horizon, weights))` — bootstrap-derived stretch for long horizons, clamped at 1 from below. At 30y factor = 1 (Trinity baseline). At 60y factor ≈ 1.4 (need ~40% more to survive sequence risk over a longer span). At horizons < 30y the raw ratio drops below 1, but we clamp so `fireTarget` never shrinks below the rule-of-thumb floor `expenses × 100/userRate` — see "Why the clamp" below.

Sliders move the bar:
- **Rate up** → smaller `baseMultiplier` → smaller target → bar up.
- **Horizon up from 30y** → bigger `horizonFactor` → bigger target → bar down.
- **Horizon down from 30y** → factor stays at 1 (clamp) → bar unchanged. Intentional: the cash-flow constraint at the chosen rate is the binding floor; no point pretending you need less money than the rule-of-thumb says.
- **Patrimony up** → numerator up → bar up.

### Why the clamp

Pre-clamp behavior produced a contradiction: at 5.5%, 20y, R$ 2M patrimony, R$ 9,839/mo expenses, the bar read 118% with "Meta atingida: R$ 1,690,197" while the cash-flow line said "Retirada R$ 9,166/mês · faltam R$ 672,75/mês". Both were correct under their own framing — bar = "patrimony covers horizon-adjusted target", gap = "withdrawal at chosen rate < expenses" — but obviously inconsistent to the user. The clamp resolves it: when the bar reads ≥100%, the chosen rate's withdrawal also covers expenses.

The lifestyle bootstrap success and the rate bootstrap success are still computed and shown as **secondary indicators** below the bar (sustainability check, sequence-risk diagnostic, depletion percentiles). They don't drive the bar — the user's "FIRE number" mental model does.

`safeRate = findSafeWithdrawalRate(targetYears, weights)` is also shown as the horizon-aware reference rate and triggers the "agressivo" warning when `userRate > safeRate`.

Color thresholds on the bar:
- ≥ 95% → green (`Colors.brand`) — Trinity-style FIRE-equivalent.
- 70–95% → amber (`Colors.brand200`).
- < 70% → red (`Colors.danger200`).

The **rule-of-thumb FIRE target** (`fireTarget = annualExpenses × 100 / withdrawalRate`) is still computed but used only for the informational "Meta de FIRE (regra X%): Y (faltam Z)" line below the bar. It tells the user the classical FIRE-number reference at their chosen rate. It does *not* drive the bar.

`safeRate = findSafeWithdrawalRate(targetYears, weights)` is shown in the hint row as a horizon-aware reference (the rate at which bootstrap success would be 95% for the chosen horizon and allocation) and triggers the "agressivo" warning when the user's rate exceeds it.

### Design history (avoid re-litigating)

The user's mental model: bar = "fraction of money I need to retire" — i.e., `patrimony / FIRE_number`. They want both sliders to monotonically move the bar via FIRE_number, with no asymmetric behavior. We landed on horizon-stretched multiplier scaling (transparent, visible in the displayed multiplier) after rejecting:

- **Option A**: `target = expenses × (100 / userRate)`. Horizon didn't move the bar.
- **Option B-old (rate as what-if)**: `target = expenses × (100 / safeRate)`. Rate slider inert.
- **Option C-cap**: `target = expenses × (100 / min(userRate, safeRate))`. Asymmetric — slider rate or horizon dominated depending on which side of safeRate.
- **Framing B with rate-based bootstrap** (bar = bootstrap success at `userRate × patrimony`): scale-invariant, patrimony didn't move the bar (user reported "96% success but I need to double my money").
- **Framing B with lifestyle bootstrap** (bar = bootstrap success at `annualExpenses`): rate slider didn't move the bar.
- **Composite** (`bar = targetCoverage × lifestyleSuccess × 100`): all three drivers moved the bar, but the bar's value didn't match the user's mental model of "fraction of FIRE_number" (a 58%-of-target user saw 33% on the bar because the success rate factor dragged it down).

**Current approach** — bar = patrimony / fireTarget, with `fireTarget = expenses × (100/userRate) × max(1, horizonFactor)`. The horizon stretch is transparent: the displayed multiplier ("regra 4% × ajuste 60a = 35×") shows both components, so it's not silent scaling. The clamp at 1 from below was added after a user-reported bug where the bar read 118% while the cash-flow line said "faltam R$ 672/mês" — see "Why the clamp" above. Above 30y both sliders monotonically move the bar via fireTarget; below 30y the horizon slider intentionally doesn't move it because the rule-of-thumb floor binds. Patrimony always moves the bar via the numerator.

**Do not** revert to a static-multiplier or a bootstrap-success bar. Both fail the user's mental model. The horizon-stretched multiplier is transparent (visible in the UI) and produces the expected behavior at every operating point.

## Lifestyle gap is shown separately

Under Framing B, the bar shows sustainability at the slider rate, *not* whether the user's actual expenses are covered. The lifestyle gap surfaces in two other places:

- **"Retirada vs Despesas"** line — always shown. Compares `monthlyWithdrawal = patrimony × rate / 12` to `avgExpenses`, with the surplus/shortfall.
- **"Meta de FIRE (regra X%): Y (faltam Z)"** line — shown when `ruleOfThumbProgress < 100`. Tells the user how far their patrimony is from the classical FIRE number at their chosen rate.

Together these convey "is this actually enough money to live?" while the bar conveys "is this rate sustainable at this horizon for this allocation?". A user is FIRE'd when both align: bar ≥ 95% AND withdrawal ≥ expenses.

## Determinism

`fireBootstrap.ts` uses **Mulberry32** seeded with a fixed constant (`FIXED_SEED = 42`), not `Math.random()`. This is intentional:

- Same inputs (patrimony, withdrawal, horizon, weights) → identical bootstrap output across reloads. Users don't see jitter.
- A fixed seed means slider movements test the *same* 2000 sampled return sequences at different parameters → smoother, more interpretable transitions in success rate.
- Using a hash of inputs as the seed was considered and rejected for the same reason — it would make small slider changes scramble the random sequences, causing non-monotonic jumps in the success rate.

## Time-varying-weights variant (Idade em RF)

`fireBootstrap.ts` exposes a second pair of entry points — `runBootstrapWithVaryingWeights` and `findSafeWithdrawalRateWithVaryingWeights` — that take a `weightsAt: (yearIndex: number) => AllocationWeights` callback instead of a fixed `AllocationWeights` object. The trial loop calls `weightsAt(y - 1)` to compute the blend for the year ending at index `y`, then draws one historical real return per asset class at those weights. Everything else (Mulberry32 seeded with `FIXED_SEED`, depletion bookkeeping, percentile bands, scale-invariance trick used by the SWR finder) is identical to the static-weight version.

`ConstantDollarAgeInBondsIndicator.tsx` is the only consumer today. The strategy says "bond% = age" — at year `y`, `bondPct = min(currentAge + y, 100) / 100` and `stockPct = 1 - bondPct`. The stock-side split between `equity` and `ifix` preserves the user's current equity:ifix ratio, since the strategy only dictates the bond/stock split and doesn't speak to which equity vehicle to hold.

### Why time-varying weights are the right model here

The age-in-bonds glide path is *intrinsic* to the strategy definition — bond% = age is what makes it the strategy, not an incidental allocation choice. Modeling it with the static `runBootstrap` would mean picking a single bond% (today's age, retirement-age, average over the horizon, etc.), and any choice misrepresents what the user is actually doing:

- Use today's age → simulation is too aggressive late in retirement (real allocation has crept toward bonds, but the bootstrap keeps drawing equity at young-person weights).
- Use end-of-horizon age → too defensive early on (the bootstrap drawn under heavy bond weights despite the user actually holding mostly equities in the early years).
- Use a midpoint → both errors at once, smaller in magnitude.

Time-varying weights eliminate the choice. The bootstrap traces the same glide path the user will actually live, which is what gives the success rate an honest interpretation.

### Caveats specific to `findSafeWithdrawalRateWithVaryingWeights`

- The "safe rate" is **trajectory-dependent**, not just horizon- and weights-dependent. Two users with the same horizon and the same starting weights but different glide-path slopes get different safe rates. So does the same user at different starting ages — a 35-year-old's 30-year safe rate under age-in-bonds is *not* the same as a 65-year-old's 30-year safe rate under age-in-bonds, because the average bond% over the simulation differs.
- This means the safe rate cannot be cached by `(horizon, weights)` like it can in the static case. The closure captured by `weightsAt` is part of the cache key. The indicator memoizes by `[currentAge, equityTotal, ifixTotal, targetYears]` instead.
- `horizonFactor = baselineSafeRate / safeRate` (used for `fireTarget`) still works the same way — both endpoints are computed via the time-varying SWR finder, and the ratio still answers "how much do I need to stretch the rule-of-thumb multiplier to survive this horizon?". The numerical magnitudes shift compared to a static-weight portfolio, but the slider semantics (longer horizon → bigger target, lower rate → bigger target) are preserved.
- The 30-year baseline is still anchored at `findSafeWithdrawalRateWithVaryingWeights(30, weightsAt)`, *not* at the static-weight baseline. Mixing them would produce a horizon factor of 1 at the wrong operating point and break monotonicity of the bar.

## Accumulation-phase variant (time-to-target forecast)

`runAccumulationBootstrap` is the forward-running counterpart to the withdrawal variants. Starting from `startingBalance`, each simulated year it adds `annualContribution`, then applies one historical real return drawn at the portfolio `weights`, and stops when the running balance crosses `target`. Used by `ConstantDollarIndicator` and `ConstantDollarAgeInBondsIndicator` to answer the user's planning question: *"given my current patrimony and average monthly savings, how long until I reach my FIRE target?"*

Result shape: `{ successRate, medianYearsToTarget, p10YearsToTarget, p90YearsToTarget, gapBands }`. `successRate` is the fraction of trials that crossed `target` within `maxYears` (default 60). Trials that never reach are excluded from the year-to-target percentile aggregates. `gapBands` are per-year percentiles of `gap = max(0, target − balance)` across trials — bands all converge to 0 by `p90YearsToTarget` and stay there. The consuming accumulation chart slices around `p90YearsToTarget + small_buffer`; past that everything is at 0.

**Two-chart split (accumulation + drawdown).** The FIRE indicators render *two* separate charts pre-FIRE, mirroring 1/N's "before vs after" structure:

1. **Acumulação chart** uses `gapBands` from `runAccumulationBootstrap`. Y-axis = gap (BRL). X-axis = projected age when `dateOfBirth` is set (`currentAge + year`), falls back to year-from-now when not. All bands shrink to zero. Three vertical reference lines mark percentile crossings, all stacked at the chart top with `position: "top"` and progressive `dy` offsets so they don't overlap when the spread is tight: `otimista · aposenta aos N` (green, dy=-34), `mediana · aposenta aos N` (green, dy=-18), `pessimista · aposenta aos N` (red, dy=-2). Smallest gap = best case = otimista → green; largest gap = worst case = pessimista → red.

2. **Aposentadoria chart** uses a separate `runBootstrap(fireTarget, annualExpenses, targetYears, weights)` call — independent of the accumulation simulation. Y-axis = balance (BRL). X-axis = projected age (`currentAge + medianYearsToTarget + retirement_year`) when DOB is available, retirement-year offset otherwise. The tooltip is `DrawdownTooltipContent`: per percentile shows `balance · withdrawal/mês` (e.g. `Pessimista (p10): R$ 1.2M · R$ 5.500/mês`), so the user reads patrimony AND income at each age in one place. Above the chart, a stats summary line: `Sucesso em Xa: Y% · Depleção mediana: Z anos · Depleção pessimista (p10): W anos`. Highest balance = best case = otimista → green; lowest = worst → red (color flip relative to the accumulation chart, but each chart is internally consistent).

The split decouples the two questions ("when do I get there?" vs. "will it last?") and avoids the per-trial-pivot complexity of stitching both phases into a single chart. Trade-off: the drawdown chart is a hypothetical seeded at exactly `fireTarget` rather than each trial's actual crossing balance — Trinity is approximately scale-invariant so this rarely matters, but it's a small honesty caveat documented at the indicator's call site.

`runAccumulationBootstrap` is responsible only for the accumulation half. The drawdown half is `runBootstrap` with the FIRE target as starting balance. Don't fold them back into one — that's what we just split apart.

**Cenarios checkboxes.** Pre-FIRE, both charts share a single set of `Otimista / Mediana / Pessimista` checkboxes (FormControlLabel + Checkbox, right-aligned, mirrors the `/assets` ROI-chart pattern with `disabled={onlyOne && showThis}` to enforce ≥1 visible). State is per-indicator. Toggling a checkbox hides the corresponding band line in both charts AND the corresponding row in both tooltips AND the corresponding vertical reference line on the Acumulação chart.

**Simulators.** Pre-FIRE the indicator exposes three what-if controls. `PatrimonySimulator` and `ExpenseSimulator` live in the slider row at the top — both are *global* in scope (PatrimonySimulator drives `effectivePatrimony` everywhere, `simulatedExpenses` drives `effectiveMonthlyExpenses` → `annualExpenses` → fireTarget → progress bar → accumulation bootstrap → drawdown bootstrap, all coherently). `SavingsSimulator` lives directly under the `Acumulação · quanto falta para a meta` caption, signaling its scope: it only feeds `runAccumulationBootstrap`'s `annualContribution`. The placement is intentional — pulling it out of the global slider row was a deliberate communication choice.

### Inverted percentile semantics

Watch out — the p10/p90 conventions flip relative to the depletion variants:

| Variant | p10 means | p90 means |
|---|---|---|
| Withdrawal depletion (`runBootstrap`) | year by which 10% of trials have depleted (success = +∞ in the sort, so this is `null` when ≥90% survive) — earliest failures | unused; we report `medianDepletionYear` for the central case |
| Accumulation (`runAccumulationBootstrap`) | best decile of years-to-target — *fastest* to reach | worst decile of years-to-target — *slowest* to reach |

Both are correct under "p10 = the 10th percentile of the metric, sorted ascending"; the meaning just flips because reaching-target faster is good, and depleting faster is bad. UI labels in the consuming indicators should be explicit ("otimista (p10)" / "pessimista (p90)" for accumulation; "Depleção pessimista (p10)" for withdrawal) — don't paste a withdrawal-style label onto an accumulation result or vice versa.

### How the indicator wires it

The FIRE indicators receive `monthlySavings` as a prop (typically `revenuesAvg − expensesAvg` from the existing 12-month rolling averages). They convert to `annualContribution = max(0, monthlySavings) × 12` and pass the same `weights` they use for the withdrawal bootstrap. Three UI states:

- **Deficit** (`monthlySavings ≤ 0`): show a danger-colored hint instead of running the projection. A median time-to-target with no contributions would still produce a number (returns alone can grow into the target if you wait long enough), but it would be misleading planning advice.
- **Reachable** (`successRate > 0` and `medianYearsToTarget !== null`): full breakdown — median, p10 (otimista), p90 (pessimista), success rate within 60 years.
- **Unreachable** (`successRate = 0` within `maxYears`): danger-colored "improvável atingir em 60 anos" hint with the historical success rate.

In compact mode (home page), only the median lands in the existing "faltam R$ X" line as "em ~Z anos no ritmo atual"; the full breakdown lives on the strategy detail page.

### Why same-`weights` accumulation isn't quite right for Idade-em-RF

`ConstantDollarAgeInBondsIndicator` uses the user's *current* allocation for the accumulation bootstrap, not the time-varying glide path. This is a deliberate honesty trade-off: pre-retirement, the user is still working and hasn't started rebalancing toward bonds, so today's mix is the correct input. The age-glide only kicks in during the *withdrawal* phase, which begins after the target is reached. If we instead drove accumulation through the age-glide, we'd be modeling allocations the user isn't actually holding yet. Documented here so future-you doesn't "fix" it.

## Things to preserve when modifying

1. **Don't switch to lifestyle framing without re-introducing slider semantics** — the user explicitly explored both; Trinity won because the slider must do something to the success rate.
2. **The `fireProgress` threshold gates *which* charts render, not whether the bootstrap runs.** Pre-FIRE we run two bootstraps (accumulation + a hypothetical drawdown seeded at `fireTarget`) and render both charts; post-FIRE we render only the existing `runBootstrap(currentPatrimony, ...)` chart. Don't collapse this: pre-FIRE the user needs the time-to-target story (Acumulação), and the Aposentadoria preview is meaningful precisely *because* it's hypothetical — it answers "if I hit the target, will it last?" The old gate ("hide the bootstrap entirely below 100%") is gone and shouldn't be reintroduced; the bootstrap is meaningful in both regimes.
3. **Don't reseed per render** — reverting to `Math.random()` will reintroduce reload jitter the user complained about.
4. **Bank cash counts as fixed income** — call sites pass `fixedIncomeTotal + bankAmount` so the allocation reflects that cash earns ~risk-free real returns, not zero.
5. **`Ação EUA` and `Cripto` are lumped into the equity bucket** — explicit simplification because we don't ship US equity / crypto historical real-return series. Document any change to this in the call sites in `Indicators.tsx`, `StrategyDetailPage.tsx`, `PlanningHub.tsx`.
6. **Data refresh**: re-running `generate_fire_returns_ts` overwrites `fireReturns.ts`. The script lives in `django/variable_income_assets/scripts.py` and is callable from the Django shell.
7. **Don't collapse the time-varying variant into the static one** — they look similar but the static version *cannot* be safely substituted for an age-in-bonds-style strategy (see "Why time-varying weights are the right model here"). Keep both pairs of functions side by side. If you need a third strategy with its own glide path, add a new `weightsAt` callback at the call site rather than threading another parameter through the static-weight path.
